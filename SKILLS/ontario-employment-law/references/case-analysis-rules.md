# Case Analysis Rules — Applying ESA to a Specific Case

## Rule 1 — Separate Fact Types

Before any analysis, sort everything into four buckets:

| Type | Definition | How to Treat |
|---|---|---|
| Confirmed Fact | Supported by a document or both parties agree | State it as fact, cite the source |
| Claimant Position | What the employee says happened | State it as their position, note it needs corroboration |
| Employer Allegation | What the employer claims | State it as allegation only, note it needs proof |
| Gap / Unknown | Needed for analysis but not available | Flag it clearly, state why it matters |

Never state an allegation as if it were proven.
Never state a claimant position as fact without corroboration.

## Rule 2 — Always Cite Your Source
Every statement in your analysis must reference:
- The document it comes from (e.g., "EV-001 states...")
- Or be clearly marked "assumed," "estimated," or "needs verification"

## Rule 3 — Employment Status First
Before calculating any amounts, assess whether the person qualifies as an employee.
Use the multi-factor test in employment-status.md.
If status is in doubt, note the risk and proceed with the assumption that they are an employee
(per ESA presumption), but flag the uncertainty.

## Rule 4 — Calculate Conservatively
1. Start with the minimum wage scenario (safest, hardest to dispute)
2. Then build an agreed-rate scenario if there is any evidence of a higher rate
3. Show each calculation step explicitly
4. Label every assumption
5. Give a range: "Between $X (minimum) and $Y (if agreed rate proven)"

Never give a single confident number if inputs are uncertain.

## Rule 5 — Risk-Rate Every Claim

| Risk Level | Meaning |
|---|---|
| High Risk | Weak or no evidence; employer has strong counter-argument |
| Medium Risk | Some evidence; outcome uncertain; needs more documentation |
| Low Risk | Strong evidence; employer would struggle to defeat this claim |

## Rule 6 — Counter-Argument Analysis
For each employer argument or allegation:
1. State the argument clearly and fairly
2. Identify what they would need to prove it
3. Assess whether they have that proof
4. Identify your counter-evidence
5. Rate the strength of their argument

## Rule 7 — Gap Analysis
After your main analysis, always produce:
- What specific evidence is still missing?
- How critical is it to the claim?
- Can the claim succeed without it?
- How could it be obtained?

## Rule 8 — Tone and Framing
- Be direct and honest, including about weaknesses
- Do not inflate the case to make the person feel better
- Frame uncertainty accurately: "appears," "based on available evidence," "subject to verification"
- Always include: "This is not legal advice — review with a licensed Ontario lawyer or paralegal before filing"

## SK Custom Home Case — Specific Analysis Notes

### Employment Status (Preliminary)

Pointing toward employee:
- Employer's own communications use the phrase "employment period" (EV-001, EV-002)
- "3-month experimental employment period" = probationary employment, not contracting
- No evidence of the worker having a registered business, multiple clients, or invoicing
- ESA s.2(1) presumption applies — employer must prove otherwise

Employer's counter-argument:
- Claims "experimental/trial" basis only (EV-003)
- Claims no formal agreement executed (EV-003)
- Claims payments were "discretionary goodwill" (EV-003)

Assessment: The employer's own termination notices undermine their "no employment" argument.
Using the word "employment period" and "termination" is a significant admission.
Medium-high confidence in employee status. Needs evidence of work instructions / control to strengthen.

### Wage Claim (Preliminary Calculation)

If worked approx 12 weeks x 44 hrs/week = approx 528 hours minimum
At $17.20/hour = approx $9,082 gross wages
Plus vacation pay (4%) = approx $363
Plus any overtime if hours exceeded 44/week consistently
Less any payments received
Plus any proven reimbursable expenses

Key unknowns: Exact hours, exact weeks, payments received — these are the priority gaps.

### Termination Pay
- Employed 3+ months = entitled to ESA termination pay
- 3 months to 1 year = 1 week minimum notice or pay in lieu
- No cause proven = likely without cause termination
- Common law notice (if using Small Claims): likely 4-8 weeks for this position and length

### Employer's $26,000 Counter-Claim
- This is an allegation only (EV-001, EV-002) — no supporting documents provided
- For ESA claim: irrelevant — employer cannot deduct alleged losses from wages owed
- For Small Claims: if they sue you, demand full financial disclosure and causation proof
- Risk level: Medium (if they actually sue) / Low (for your ESA claim)
