# Employment Status — Ontario Legal Test

## The Core Question
Is this person an employee under the Ontario Employment Standards Act, 2000?

## Why It Matters
- ESA only protects employees (and dependent contractors in some cases)
- Employers often misclassify employees as contractors to avoid ESA obligations
- The ESA deems ambiguous cases in favour of employee status

## The Multi-Factor Test (Ontario Courts & Ministry)

Ontario uses a holistic multi-factor test. No single factor is determinative.
The overall picture of the relationship is what matters.

### Factor 1 — Control
Employee indicators:
- Employer sets hours, location, methods, dress code
- Employer can discipline or direct work
- Worker cannot subcontract or send a substitute

Contractor indicators:
- Worker sets own hours and methods
- Worker can hire others to do the work
- No supervision over how work is done

### Factor 2 — Ownership of Tools & Equipment
Employee indicators:
- Employer provides tools, vehicles, materials, technology
- Worker uses employer's facilities

Contractor indicators:
- Worker provides own tools, vehicle, equipment
- Worker has invested capital in their business

### Factor 3 — Chance of Profit / Risk of Loss
Employee indicators:
- Worker earns a fixed wage or salary regardless of business outcome
- Worker has no financial risk if the job goes badly

Contractor indicators:
- Worker can profit more by being efficient
- Worker bears the cost of mistakes or delays

### Factor 4 — Integration
Employee indicators:
- Worker's work is integral to the employer's core business
- Worker is part of the employer's organization

Contractor indicators:
- Worker is an accessory to the business, not integral
- Worker operates an independent business with multiple clients

### Factor 5 — Exclusivity
Employee indicators:
- Worker works only for this employer (or primarily)
- Worker cannot work for competitors

Contractor indicators:
- Worker has multiple clients
- Worker markets their services independently

### Factor 6 — Written Agreement
- Presence of an "independent contractor" agreement points toward contractor
- BUT courts will look past labels to the real relationship
- Absence of any agreement often points toward employee

### Factor 7 — Economic Dependence
Employee indicators:
- Worker is financially dependent on this employer
- Worker has no independent business infrastructure

---

## ESA Statutory Presumption

Under ESA s.2(1): if a person performs work for an employer, they are presumed to be an employee unless the employer can prove otherwise.
The burden is on the employer to prove the worker is NOT an employee.

---

## Dependent Contractor
A middle category exists: the dependent contractor.
Even if not a full employee, a dependent contractor who is economically dependent
on one employer may be entitled to reasonable notice on termination.
This is a common law concept, not ESA — but worth noting.

---

## How to Apply This to a Case

1. List all facts about the relationship under each factor
2. Note which factors point employee vs contractor
3. Assess the overall picture
4. If ambiguous, ESA presumption favours employee
5. Note what additional evidence would strengthen the analysis

---

## Key Case Principles
- Sagaz Industries (SCC 2001): Established the multi-factor test
- McKee v Reid's Heritage Homes (Ont CA 2009): Economic dependency = dependent contractor
- The word "experimental" or "trial" in a termination notice does NOT make someone a non-employee — it describes a probationary period, which is still employment
