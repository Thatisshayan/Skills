# Ontario Small Claims Court — Employment Disputes

## When to Use Small Claims vs. ESA

| Factor | ESA (Ministry) | Small Claims Court |
|---|---|---|
| Filing fee | Free | $102-$215 |
| Maximum claim | No cap | $35,000 |
| Who investigates | Ministry officer | You (self-represented or lawyer) |
| Speed | Slower | Faster (you control timeline) |
| Common law notice | Not available | Yes (often larger than ESA) |
| Legal costs if you win | No | Sometimes awarded |

Key Rule: You can generally file EITHER an ESA claim OR a Small Claims claim for the same issue — not both.
However, ESA claims and common law notice claims can sometimes run in parallel if amounts don't overlap.

## Small Claims Court Basics

- Court: Ontario Superior Court of Justice (Small Claims Division)
- Maximum claim: $35,000 (not including interest and costs)
- Self-represented: Allowed and common
- Paralegal: Can represent you at Small Claims

## Filing Process

### Step 1 — Prepare Your Plaintiff's Claim (Form 7A)

The Plaintiff's Claim must include:
- Your name and address (plaintiff)
- The defendant's full legal name and address
- Amount claimed
- Basis for the claim (what happened, what they owe, why)

Download Form 7A: https://www.ontariocourts.ca/scj/civil/forms/

### Step 2 — File at the Court

File at the Small Claims Court in the jurisdiction where:
- The defendant lives or carries on business, OR
- The event that gave rise to the claim occurred

Filing fees:
- Claims up to $10,000: $102
- Claims over $10,000: $215

### Step 3 — Serve the Defendant
- Serve within 6 months of filing
- File Proof of Service (Form 8A) with the court after serving

### Step 4 — Defendant's Response
- Defendant has 20 days to file a Defence (Form 9A)
- If defendant doesn't respond: request Default Judgment
- If defendant responds: case proceeds to Settlement Conference

### Step 5 — Settlement Conference
- Mandatory before trial
- A judge helps the parties try to settle
- Many cases settle here

### Step 6 — Trial (If No Settlement)
- Bring 3 copies of all documents (organized in tabs)
- You present your case first (you are the plaintiff)
- Judge issues decision

## What You Can Claim (Employment Context)

| Claim Type | Includable? | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Unpaid wages | Yes | All hours worked x rate |
| Overtime | Yes | Hours over 44/week x 1.5x |
| Vacation pay | Yes | 4% or 6% of gross |
| Expense reimbursements | Yes | With receipts |
| Termination pay (ESA) | Yes | Based on length of service |
| Common law notice | Yes | Often larger than ESA — key advantage |
| Interest | Yes | Pre-judgment interest (Courts of Justice Act rate) |

## Common Law Notice vs. ESA Notice

ESA Minimum Notice: Set by statute (see esa-core.md)
Common Law Reasonable Notice: Set by courts based on:
- Age of employee
- Length of service
- Character of position (seniority)
- Availability of similar employment

Common law notice is almost always longer than ESA notice.
For short employment, even 3 months can warrant 4-8 weeks of common law notice.

---

## Limitation Period
2 years from the date the cause of action arose.
For terminated employees: 2 years from the termination date.
For unpaid wages: 2 years from when each wage payment was due.

---

## Tips for Self-Represented Claimants

1. Be concise and factual in your Plaintiff's Claim
2. Organize your evidence in a binder with numbered tabs
3. Stick to amounts you can prove — inflated claims hurt credibility
4. Bring calculations showing how you arrived at the amount
5. Prepare a brief outline of your story (1-2 pages)
6. Do not be emotional — state facts, reference documents
7. Attend the Settlement Conference seriously — most cases settle there
